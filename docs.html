PULSE Docs • Real Estate & Financing

Protocol Documentation

This documentation explains how PULSE is intended to work: pool structure, cashflow routing, reporting, staking and governance. PULSE focuses primarily on real estate and financing RWAs, while remaining extensible to other RWA categories.

What you get

Clear mechanics and a disclosure-first approach.

What we avoid

No “guaranteed yield” claims. No fake partners.

Status

Pre‑launch.

1) Protocol Architecture (High-level)

PULSE is designed around a simple flow: pools define terms, cashflows occur off-chain, and distribution is routed on-chain according to published rules.

Pool Registry

Lists pools and their parameters: duration, caps, payout schedule, fees (if any), and disclosures.

Cashflow Router

Routes distributions to defined recipients (LPs, stakers, treasury) based on pool rules.

Reporting Layer

Publishes periodic updates and links to references where available for allocations and events.

Staking & Governance

Mechanisms to align long-term holders and enable community-controlled parameter adjustments over time.

2) RWA Pools (Real Estate & Financing)

Pools are the core product. Each pool should be accompanied by clear, user-facing terms and risk disclosures.

  • Real estate pools: structured income streams (e.g., income notes, rent-like distributions, cashflow notes).
  • Financing pools: short-duration exposures (e.g., invoice financing, credit-like pools with defined schedules).
  • Diversified baskets: multi-asset pools to reduce concentration.

3) Cashflow Routing (From off-chain to on-chain)

In an RWA protocol, cashflows originate off-chain and must be reflected on-chain in a way that is verifiable, consistent, and aligned with pool terms.

Cashflow Event

Interest payment, repayment, rent-like distribution, or structured payout occurs off-chain.

Validation & Update

Event is validated and recorded in the reporting layer. References may be provided where possible.

On-chain Distribution

Router allocates funds based on rules: LPs / stakers / treasury, following the pool schedule.

Post-Event Reporting

Summary: what happened, how much was routed, any exceptions, and next scheduled payout.

4) Transparency & Reporting

Trust comes from consistent reporting. A mature reporting approach includes:

  • Pool snapshots: allocation, caps, and key parameters.
  • Performance notes: material changes, delays, events, and reasons.
  • Distribution records: on-chain distribution references where possible.
  • Disclosure updates: changes to risk profile or terms must be clearly communicated.

5) PLS Token Utility (Governance + Staking)

PLS is intended to power protocol participation. Utility should be explicitly documented:

  • Governance: proposals and voting for parameters (pool caps, incentives, treasury allocations).
  • Staking: participation mechanism; reward sources and rules published before activation.
  • Incentives: time-bound programs for liquidity bootstrapping, disclosed and measurable.

6) Staking (Planned)

Staking becomes meaningful when it has clear rules and transparent sources for rewards.

7) Governance (Planned)

Governance should start simple and expand. A clean v1 governance model usually includes:

  • Proposal types: parameter changes, new pool onboarding, incentive budgets
  • Voting windows: clear start/end times and quorum rules
  • Execution: transparent implementation path (on-chain execution or multi-sig with disclosure)
  • Scope boundaries: what governance cannot do (safety limits)

8) Fees & Treasury

Fees (if any) must be unambiguous. Disclose the type, destination, and timing of any fees used by the protocol.

9) Security Model

Security is a process. Typical elements include:

  • Testing and staged releases (devnet → limited mainnet → full release)
  • Code review and audit links (when completed)
  • Monitoring and incident response plan
  • Responsible disclosure / bug bounty (when applicable)

10) Risk Disclosures

RWAs + DeFi include multiple risk categories:

  • Smart contract risk: bugs, exploits, upgrades
  • Counterparty/operational risk: off-chain processes, servicing, payments
  • Market risk: changes in asset value, liquidity conditions
  • Regulatory risk: availability varies by jurisdiction

11) Wallets & UX

PULSE uses standard Solana wallet provider flows (e.g., Phantom-compatible). Good UX and safety guidelines:

  • Never ask for seed phrases
  • Verify the domain before connecting
  • Confirm transactions in wallet UI, not in popups
  • Use small amounts for first interactions

12) FAQ (Docs)

Is yield guaranteed?

No. Returns are not guaranteed. Pool performance depends on underlying cashflows and risk events.

When will staking be live?

Staking is planned to be activated after token launch, with rules published prior to activation.

How do I evaluate a pool?

Read the pool terms: duration, caps, payout schedule, fees, disclosures, and reporting cadence.

How is transparency provided?

Through periodic reporting, disclosures, and on-chain distribution references where feasible.

13) Glossary

RWA — Real‑World Asset, an off-chain asset that can generate cashflows.
Pool — A set of terms that define exposure, duration, caps, and payout rules.
Cashflow — A payment event (interest, repayment, income) originating off-chain.
Router — On-chain mechanism that distributes funds based on pool rules.
Disclosure — Publicly documented risks, terms, and material changes.
Staking — Locking tokens for incentives and/or participation.
Governance — Proposals and voting to change protocol parameters within defined limits.
